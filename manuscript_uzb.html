Population Genomics of Uzbeks: Genome-wide Insights from a 1,047-individual Cohort

Manuscript draft — Population genetics of Central Asia (Paper 1 of 2)

Draft v0.1 · Apr 2026 Target: Genome Biology / Eur J Hum Genet / Genome Med ~5,000 words target

Title & Abstract

Working title

“Genome-wide characterisation of the Uzbek population reveals South-Asian/East-European admixture, low inbreeding, and selection signatures at immune and metabolic loci”

Abstract (250 words, structured)

Background

Central Asian populations remain under-represented in human genomic resources. Existing reference panels (1000 Genomes, gnomAD) contain few or no Uzbek samples, limiting variant frequency estimation, imputation accuracy, and clinical interpretation in this population of ~36 million. Comparative studies place Uzbeks at the cline between European, South Asian, and East Asian ancestries (Yunusbayev et al. 2012; Jeong et al. 2019), but high-resolution autosomal data have not been published.

Methods

We genotyped 1,247 Uzbek individuals on a custom Illumina array, applied a multi-stage QC pipeline (sample missingness, IBD deduplication, variant call rate, HWE), imputed against the TOPMed r2 reference (Michigan Imputation Server, Eagle v2.4 + Minimac4), and merged the resulting 1,074-sample high-quality dataset with all 2,548 samples of the 1000 Genomes Phase 3 release. We ran principal component analysis, ADMIXTURE (K=2–8), unsupervised sNMF (validation), pairwise Weir & Cockerham FST, classical multidimensional scaling, the Population Branch Statistic (PBS), Ensembl Variant Effect Predictor annotation, runs-of-homozygosity analysis, and IBD-based relatedness.

Results

Uzbeks form a single genetic cluster intermediate between European and South Asian populations, with weighted FST=0.018 against SAS and 0.020 against EUR; FST=0.060 vs AFR. ADMIXTURE K=5 best partitions the global panel; within Uzbeks, K=2 is parsimonious and follows a geographic East–West cline that explains 8.2% of variance. PBS analysis identifies 8 lead variants (PBS ≥ 0.3) and 490 candidates after relaxation, enriching for HLA/MHC genes (chr 6 — 33.7% of independent loci) and immune/metabolic functions; 5 are missense including damaging variants in SPI1 and SLC6A2. Median FROH=0.015, comparable to outbred European populations and distinctly lower than reported values for some other Central Asian groups; only 0.08% of pairs exceed PI_HAT 0.0884.

Conclusion

This work establishes the first medium-scale, openly described autosomal reference for Uzbeks, refines their position within global human genetic variation, identifies population-specific selection candidates, and provides imputation-quality summary statistics for downstream biomedical genomics in Central Asia.

1. Introduction

Two narrative arcs to weave together: (1) Central Asia as a missing piece of the global genomic map, (2) the methodological case for adding Uzbeks specifically — geographic position, sample feasibility, biomedical relevance.

1.1. The Central Asia gap in human genomics

  • Population-genomic literature on Central Asia is dominated by Y-chromosome / mtDNA studies (Wells et al. 2001; Heyer et al. 2009; Karafet et al. 2018); autosomal SNP arrays are rarer.
  • Existing autosomal references include the HGDP (small Uyghur and Hazara samples; Bergstrom et al. 2020), Yunusbayev et al. 2012 (~600,000 SNPs across multiple Turkic groups, including a small Uzbek subset), and the SGDP (Mallick et al. 2016, single-digit Uzbek samples).
  • The 1000 Genomes Phase 3 release contains no Uzbek samples; gnomAD v3.1 reports no Central Asian super-population.
  • Consequence: imputation accuracy in Central Asia is reduced, allele-frequency estimates for clinical variants are extrapolated, and association studies cannot use a population-matched reference.

1.2. Why Uzbeks specifically?

  • Demographic weight: ~36 million people, the largest Central Asian nationality.
  • Geographic centrality: spans East–West Silk Road; a natural test case for ancient admixture.
  • Mixed ancestry expectation: prior work places them between EUR and EAS; this study quantifies the proportions and tests whether structure correlates with internal regional provenance.

1.3. Aims

  1. Provide a publicly described 1,047-sample autosomal reference for Uzbeks at imputation-grade SNP density (post-imputation 9.9M well-imputed variants).
  2. Position the Uzbek genetic background in global ancestry space using PCA, FST, and ADMIXTURE.
  3. Identify population-specific selection signatures using PBS with EUR + EAS outgroups.
  4. Quantify endogamy and relatedness via ROH and IBD.

2.2. Genotyping & QC

DNA from 1,247 self-identified Uzbek volunteers was genotyped on the [array name] following the manufacturer’s protocol. Quality control was performed in PLINK 1.9 (Chang et al. 2015): sample missingness > 0.20 (n=92 removed; 1,155 retained), IBD deduplication (PI_HAT ≥ 0.98 for monozygotic / sample duplicates; 57 removed; 1,098 retained), SNP call rate < 0.10, MAF < 0.01, HWE p < 10−6 (654K → 473K variants). See Step 1–Step 3.

2.3. Imputation

Eagle v2.4 phasing followed by Minimac4 imputation against TOPMed r2 was performed on the Michigan Imputation Server. Post-imputation we filtered on Rsq ≥ 0.3, MAF ≥ 0.01, HWE p < 10−6 in controls, retaining 9,981,814 SNPs across 1,074 samples; 16 samples were lost to ID-merge failures and the final analytic dataset comprised 1,047 individuals after population QC. See Step 4– Step 6.

2.4. Reference panel merging

Variants were intersected by chromosomal position with the 1000 Genomes Phase 3 reference (2,548 samples after liftover to GRCh38), restricted to overlapping bi-allelic SNPs and LD-pruned (PLINK --indep-pairwise 50 5 0.2) yielding ~376,000 SNPs for ADMIXTURE and ~88,000 for population statistics.

2.5. PCA & ADMIXTURE

PCA was computed with PLINK2; the first two PCs separate Uzbeks from EUR/SAS/EAS axes. ADMIXTURE 1.3 was run for K=2–8 with 10-fold cross-validation; sNMF (Frichot et al. 2014) was used as a model-class robustness check. Within-Uzbek structure was assessed at K=2–5 on the Uzbek-only LD-pruned subset.

2.6. FST & MDS

Pairwise Weir & Cockerham FST (Weir & Cockerham 1984) was estimated with PLINK --fst across 5 super-populations (UZB, EUR, EAS, SAS, AFR); MDS in 2 dimensions was computed on the FST distance matrix.

2.7. PBS & functional annotation

PBS (Yi et al. 2010) was computed with EUR and EAS as outgroups, treating Uzbek as the focal branch. The 8 SNPs with PBS ≥ 0.3 (and 490 with PBS ≥ 0.15 after pruning) were annotated with Ensembl VEP 110 against GENCODE 44 and cross-referenced to GTEx v8 eQTLs and the GWAS Catalog.

2.8. Runs of homozygosity & relatedness

ROH segments were called with PLINK --homozyg (window 50 SNPs, density 50 kb, minimum 1 Mb). FROH was computed as total ROH length / autosomal genome length (2,881,033 kb GRCh38). IBD/PI_HAT was estimated with PLINK on the LD-pruned dataset; pairs with PI_HAT > 0.0884 (loosely 3rd-degree relatives) were flagged.

3.2. PCA & global FST

Projected onto the 1000 Genomes PC space, Uzbeks form a tight cluster intermediate between EUR and SAS along PC1, with mild spread along PC2 toward EAS. Pairwise FST places UZB closest to SAS (0.018), then EUR (0.020), EAS (0.039), and AFR (0.060) — values within published ranges for between-continent comparisons (1000 Genomes Consortium 2015).

3.3. ADMIXTURE

Cross-validation supports K=5 as the most parsimonious global model (CVK=5=0.294, plateau through K=8). Uzbeks consistently express a mixture of a Western (EUR-like) and an Eastern (CAS/SAS-like) component, with smaller fractions of East-Asian and African ancestry. Within Uzbeks alone, K=2 is best (CV monotonically increases K=2→8); the K=2 Western component (Q1) varies smoothly across geographic regions and explains 8.2% of birthplace variance (ANOVA, p < 10−15), refuting the hypothesis of discrete sub-populations.

3.4. PBS selection candidates

Eight lead variants had PBS ≥ 0.3 (Table 2). After LD clumping (Step 13) the broader candidate set (PBS ≥ 0.15) contained 401 independent loci across all 22 autosomes; 33.7% (135) localised to chr 6 (HLA/MHC). Five candidates were missense (SPI1, SLC6A2, TNXB, two further in HLA region); four were predicted damaging by SIFT & PolyPhen. Twelve candidates overlapped GTEx eQTLs in immune tissues. None of the 8 lead PBS variants had a prior GWAS Catalog or ClinVar entry.

[Insert Fig. 5: PBS Manhattan; Table 2: top 8 PBS candidates with annotation]

3.5. ROH & relatedness

36,702 ROH segments ≥ 1 Mb were detected across 1,047 individuals; median FROH=0.015, consistent with outbred populations and distinctly lower than ROH-rich groups such as isolated Pakistani or Saudi cohorts (Pemberton et al. 2012). PI_HAT > 0.0884 was observed for only 0.08% of all pairs (428 of 547,581), supporting the use of an unrelated subset for GWAS.

4. Discussion

4.1. Position of Uzbeks in global variation

Our FST estimates and admixture proportions reproduce the East–West gradient described in earlier sparse-marker studies (Yunusbayev et al. 2012; Jeong et al. 2019) at higher resolution. The proximity to South Asians (FST=0.018) is closer than the long-standing assumption that Central Asia is primarily “between EUR and EAS”, and is consistent with archaeogenomic evidence of Indo-Iranian gene flow (Narasimhan et al. 2019).

4.2. Selection signatures

HLA/MHC enrichment among PBS candidates is a recurring feature of selection scans in inter-cluster human populations (Sabeti et al. 2007; Bersaglieri et al. 2004) and consistent with pathogen-driven balancing selection. The non-HLA candidates — SPI1 (haematopoiesis), SLC6A2 (norepinephrine transporter), SLC6A3, TNXB — nominate immune and metabolic processes for follow-up.

4.3. Endogamy

The low FROH contrasts with anecdotal expectations of high consanguinity in Central Asia and parallels the urban/peri-urban recruitment of this cohort. Geographic stratification of FROH within Uzbekistan is likely and should be the subject of a focused follow-up.

4.4. Limitations

  • Single-array genotyping introduces ascertainment bias; whole-genome sequencing of a subset is the obvious next step.
  • Recruitment from a limited number of clinical sites may not capture rural diversity.
  • Imputation against TOPMed (Anglo-American-dominant reference) inflates uncertainty for rare Central-Asian-specific variants.
