Genome-wide Association Study of Recurrent Pregnancy Loss in Uzbek Women

Manuscript draft — Reproductive genetics in Central Asia (Paper 2 of 2)

Draft v0.1 · Apr 2026 Target: Hum Reprod / Hum Reprod Update / J Med Genet ~4,500 words target

Title & Abstract

Working title

“A first-pass genome-wide association study of recurrent pregnancy loss in 273 Uzbek cases and 509 controls: descriptive frequencies, suggestive signals, and replication feasibility for Central Asia”

Abstract (250 words, structured)

Background

Recurrent pregnancy loss (RPL) affects 1–5% of women attempting conception. Most of its genetic dissection has been performed in European-ancestry cohorts (Kolte et al. 2015; ESHRE Guideline 2017). Allele frequencies of established RPL risk variants in Central Asia are unknown, and no GWAS has been conducted in any Central Asian population.

Methods

Of 1,815 self-identified Uzbek women in the ALSU clinical registry, 1,011 were genotyped on a custom Illumina array, imputed against the TOPMed r2 reference. Following ESHRE-aligned classification (≥2 intrauterine losses at GA < 20 weeks as cases, parous women with no losses as controls), 273 cases and 509 controls entered the analytic dataset. We applied a three-tier strategy in PLINK2 with Firth-hybrid logistic regression: (Tier 1) targeted test of 8 Uzbek-specific selection candidates from a paired population genomics study (this cohort, paper 1); (Tier 2) screen of 4 known European RPL variants (F5 Leiden, F2 G20210A, MTHFR C677T, PAI-1 4G/5G); (Tier 3) genome-wide scan over 9.92 million well-imputed SNPs with covariates AGE, BMI, and three principal components. Four sensitivity analyses tested alternate case definitions (strict ESHRE ≥3 losses; including REVIEW samples; including sporadic-loss GRAY samples; including probable cases with missing gestational age).

Results

No variant reached genome-wide significance. The genomic inflation factor was λGC=0.978, indicating well-controlled population structure. The strongest signal was a cluster of correlated SNPs at chr18:70.47–70.49 Mb in the NETO1 locus (lead SNPs rs4891860/rs11366565/rs4243320/rs4243321; OR≈0.35, p≈3.2×10−7), suggesting a protective effect; 198 variants reached the suggestive threshold p < 1×10−5. None of the 8 Uzbek-specific PBS candidates was associated with RPL after Bonferroni correction. The classical thrombophilia panel did not replicate (all p > 0.6), although Uzbek allele frequencies for these variants are reported here for the first time and were 5–15 % lower than in European populations. The four sensitivity analyses confirmed the lack of a genome-wide significant signal under broader or stricter case definitions.

Conclusion

This work delivers (i) a transparent, well-calibrated first GWAS of RPL in Central Asia, (ii) Uzbek-specific allele frequencies for canonical thrombophilia variants, and (iii) a suggestive locus at NETO1 as a candidate for replication in larger cohorts. Sample size remains the dominant limitation; the dataset and summary statistics are released as a starting point for trans-ethnic meta-analysis.

1. Introduction

1.1. Recurrent pregnancy loss: clinical and genetic context

  • RPL is heterogeneous: chromosomal abnormalities (~50 % of sporadic miscarriages), anti-phospholipid syndrome, thrombophilia, uterine anatomy, endocrine dysfunction, and unexplained immune factors all contribute (ESHRE Guideline 2017).
  • Heritability has been estimated at 20–30 % in twin studies; the largest genome-wide meta-analysis to date (Kolte et al. 2020, ~10K cases) identified SYCP2L and TLE1 as genome-wide significant loci.
  • Older candidate-gene literature implicates F5 Leiden, F2 G20210A, MTHFR C677T, and PAI-1 4G/5G; meta-analyses report modest effect sizes (OR 1.2–3.5, Sergi 2014; Practice Committee ASRM 2012); robust replication has been mixed.

1.2. The trans-ethnic gap

  • All published RPL GWAS recruited European-ancestry participants.
  • For populations on the EUR–SAS–EAS cline (such as Uzbeks; see paper 1), allele frequencies and LD patterns differ; effect sizes do not necessarily transport.
  • A first descriptive GWAS in any non-European population — even underpowered — provides baseline frequencies, calibrated lambda, and Manhattan/QQ statistics that meta-analyses can ingest.

1.3. Aims of this study

  1. Provide the first GWAS of RPL in any Central Asian population.
  2. Test selection candidates from a paired Uzbek population-genomics study (paper 1) for association with RPL.
  3. Screen four classical European RPL variants and report their Uzbek allele frequencies.
  4. Quantify robustness via four pre-specified sensitivity analyses on case definition.
  5. Release calibrated genome-wide summary statistics for downstream meta-analysis.

2. Methods

2.1. Cohort & phenotype

The ALSU registry contains structured pregnancy histories for 1,815 Uzbek women (median age [X] years). Each pregnancy is coded by outcome (live birth=1, intrauterine loss=2/3/6, stillbirth or other=4, ectopic=5, medical termination=7, current pregnancy=8) and gestational age (GA) at outcome. The classification is intentionally aligned with the ESHRE 2017 guideline that defines RPL as ≥2 demonstrated pregnancy losses; we use < 20 weeks GA to exclude probable stillbirths.

2.2. Genotypes

The same 1,074-sample post-imputation HQ dataset described in paper 1 (TOPMed r2, Rsq ≥ 0.3, MAF ≥ 0.01) was the genotype source. After phenotype join, 782 individuals (273 + 509) entered the GWAS. PLINK2 v2.00a6LM (Chang et al. 2015) was used for all association testing.

2.3. Covariates

Per-individual covariates: maternal AGE at enrolment, body mass index (BMI), and the first three principal components from a within-Uzbek PCA (Step 7). ADMIXTURE K=2 ancestry (Q1) and FROH were originally proposed; Q1 was excluded for collinearity with PC1 (variance inflation factor too high) and FROH could not be joined to GWAS sample IDs in time for the primary run, leaving the model logit(RPL) ~ SNP + AGE + BMI + PC1 + PC2 + PC3. The genomic-inflation factor λGC=0.978 confirms adequate control of population structure.

2.4. Three-tier analysis

Tier 1. 8 SNPs with PBS ≥ 0.3 from the paired population-genomics study (paper 1) tested at Bonferroni threshold p < 6.25×10−3. The hypothesis is that Uzbek-specific selection signals overlap reproductive fitness.

Tier 2. 4 well-known thrombophilia / one-carbon-metabolism RPL variants (rs6025 F5 Leiden, rs1799963 F2 G20210A, rs1801133 MTHFR C677T, rs1799889 PAI-1 4G/5G); Uzbek allele frequencies reported regardless of statistical significance.

Tier 3. Genome-wide Firth-hybrid logistic regression over 9,920,024 valid SNPs (after PLINK’s internal MAF/missingness filtering at run-time). Standard significance threshold p < 5×10−8; suggestive threshold p <  1×10−5.

3. Results

3.1. Analytic cohort

The 782-sample analytic dataset (273 cases + 509 controls) had a case:control ratio of 1:1.86. Median age was higher in cases than controls (Mann-Whitney p = [X]), BMI distributions overlapped, and birthplace was distributed across all 14 Uzbek administrative regions. Power calculations indicated >80% power for OR ≥ 3.0 at MAF 0.10 at the suggestive threshold; the genome-wide threshold required OR ≥ 3.0 at common variants — realistic only for thrombophilia-magnitude effects.

3.2. Tier 1 — PBS candidates

None of the 8 Uzbek-specific PBS candidates reached Bonferroni significance; smallest p > 0.25, all OR confidence intervals span 1.0. This is a negative result for the selection ↔ reproductive-fitness hypothesis at this sample size.

3.3. Tier 2 — Known European RPL variants

Three of four variants were typed; the PAI-1 indel was filtered by the imputation panel. None of the three replicated the published European odds ratios (F5 Leiden p=0.87, F2 G20210A p=0.66, MTHFR C677T p=0.66) — expected given that >1,000 cases are required for 80% power. Uzbek allele frequencies were 5–15 % lower than EUR for thrombophilia variants and within EUR ranges for MTHFR (Table X), broadly consistent with the lower published RPL prevalence in Central Asia.

3.4. Tier 3 — Genome-wide scan

The QQ plot follows the diagonal closely (λGC=0.978; no inflation, slight deflation typical of small-sample Firth correction). No variant exceeded p < 5×10−8; 198 variants reached p < 1×10−5. The strongest cluster localised to chr18:70.47–70.49 Mb within NETO1 (Neuropilin and tolloid-like 1), four LD-correlated SNPs (rs4891860, rs11366565, rs4243320, rs4243321) at A1_FREQ ≈ 0.13 with OR ≈ 0.35 and p ≈ 3.2×10−7. NETO1 has neurodevelopmental functions reported in the literature; its role in pregnancy maintenance, if any, is novel and would require replication.

4. Discussion

4.1. What this study contributes

Three modest but defendable contributions:

  1. First RPL GWAS in Central Asia. Even a null result with a calibrated lambda and a publicly released summary-statistics file is a citable resource for trans-ethnic meta-analysis (e.g., the next iteration of Kolte et al. 2020).
  2. Uzbek allele frequencies for European thrombophilia variants. These have clinical-genetics utility independent of association testing.
  3. A suggestive locus (NETO1) for replication. Although p > 5×10−8 and the gene is not an obvious candidate, an OR of 0.35 at MAF 13% is biologically interesting if confirmed.

4.2. Why no genome-wide signal

Power is the dominant explanation. Even Kolte et al. 2020, with ~10K cases, detected only two loci. With 273 cases the minimum detectable OR at α=5×10−8 is ≥3 even for common variants. The lack of significant findings should not be over-interpreted.

4.3. Why thrombophilia variants did not replicate

Lower allele frequencies in Uzbeks, smaller sample size, and lower true effect sizes than the biased earlier European meta-analyses are all consistent with the data. Publication bias in the historical thrombophilia literature is well documented (Sergi et al. 2014).

4.4. Limitations

  • Sample size: 273 cases is below the threshold for de-novo locus discovery.
  • Phenotype heterogeneity: RPL aetiology is broad, and we lack data on karyotype of products of conception, anti-phospholipid status, or uterine imaging.
  • Single-array genotyping; trans-ethnic imputation against TOPMed inflates uncertainty for rare variants.
  • FROH not included in primary model (planned for v2).

4.5. Outlook

  • Combine with the paired population-genomics paper (paper 1) for an integrated Central Asian Reproductive Genomics resource.
  • Submit summary statistics to the GWAS Catalog and PGC for meta-analysis.
  • Recruit an additional cohort of ~500 cases through partner clinics.
  • Refine phenotype with karyotyping of recurrent loss tissue (already in pilot).
